AI, Blockchains and the Future of Law

MIT Connection Science

Workshop - MIT Media Lab, October 31, 2018

INSPIRATIONAL/INFORMAL NOTES

On October 31, 2018, MIT Connection Science hosted a gathering of lawyers involved in innovation to discuss the future of lawyering in our increasingly automated world.  Advances in technology, including artificial intelligence, pervasive computing (IoT) and cryptographically-verified interactions (blockchains et al.), are transforming the landscape of business, finance and government.  We discussed impacts on law and lawyering, and the contributions that law and lawyers can make.

Our discussions were organized as four sessions, each devoted to a particular subject, and each addressed by four breakout groups – sixteen breakout groups in all.  Each breakout group had a “firestarter” who initiated the discussion, and a student from Brown University who took notes. Under Chatham House rules, there is no attribution of a comment to a person.

 

The themes of the four sessions covered (A) the business of law, (B) the capabilities of automated transacting systems, (C) the role of humans in consent and enforcement and (D) the education of the next generation.  Following the notes, there is a rough Synthesis.

Synthesis and Indicators for Further Work

The following is a very rough synthesis of some of the discussions.  

1.        Implications for the business of law: is law like software?

Firestarters: Stacey Dogan, Dazza Greenwood, George Howard and Gabriel Teninbaum.

Both law and software are fields where text governs transactions, but the two fields have very different economic structures and use very different tools.   Are these destined to converge?  What business models will survive and emerge as everything moves online?

Discussion included themes of the limits imposed by regulation of the finances of law firms; the fee arrangements; the issue of “scaling” solutions; the possibility of using technology to remove most of the special handling, and therefore most of the legal work, from marketplaces; the advantage that large data pools give in making predictions and likely disadvantage of smaller actors.  The appearance of new entrants such as legal outsourcing companies, customer relationship management and legal workflow software, and big-data driven analytics is impacting legal business models.

2.        Consent and Enforcement of programmable legal constructs

Firestarters: Robert Craig, Oliver Goodenough, Cameron Kerry and Julia Neagu.  In an automated system, the human decisions can be understood as matters of consent (deciding for yourself) and enforcement (deciding for others).  

Consent is the direct decision by participants, for instance to offer products or services, or to purchase them.  Enforcement is the decision by others regarding consequences when the participants or third parties disagree.  Regulation falls between these two; it can include formal governmental regulation and informal regulation through risk-allocation mechanisms such as insurance and social mechanisms such as comments and ratings.  Most transactions can be decided by the parties and implemented by machines or rules.  Many incidents of enforcement can be automated, as with return of a product or loss of a security deposit.  But enforcement often involves disputes or novel circumstances that may not have been anticipated by the parties or by the system.  It will often be important to involve human judgment – for instance an arbitrator or judge.  In some circumstances a human decision is also legally required.  Similarly, in designing a system and regulating it, human decisions will often be essential. The impact of broad scale digitization and the ability of machines to implement these decisions and to infer others will reduce the scope of human intervention at all levels except the individual decisions of the parties.  As the design of the systems increasingly comes to embody rules of relationships, the law will increasingly need to rise to the challenge of “Code is Law” posed by Professor Lessig.

3.        Capabilities & Limitations of Programmable Legal Constructs

James Hazard, Anjlee Khurana, Christoph Pereira, Joyce Searls.  What parts of “legal” can be expressed in constructs that machines can understand, and what parts cannot or should not be handled by machines?

Much of routine transacting is already handled entirely via automation with rare resort to terms of use or human intervention.  Many legal concepts can be expressed as logic.  The availability of data sets permit machines to infer what humans do in similar circumstances.  It is possible to make major improvements in efficiency and access by expressing common elements as standards.  The combination of logic (code) and legal standards is often described as “Ricardian contracts,” a way to combine the advantages of machine-readable logic with legal standards.  AI systems can analyze precedents even when unstructured, and can conduct many negotiations.  The transition to standards and automation can be incremental.  Standards can be suggested, differences compared, and exceptions integrated as additional standards.

There is a residual but critical part of law that cannot or should not be automated, that expresses principles and human arrangements for governance.  Standards can shift power from large organizations to individuals and smaller groups.  Standards could also permit individuals and others to have integrated databases of their own, giving a full picture of all of their transactions.

4.        Preparing & Training the Next Generation

Who should be doing law?  What should lawyers learn in school?  As decisions are increasingly made by based on machine logic or data sets, what should aspiring lawyers learn?  What law should coders learn?  Do data-oriented studies, such as public health, have things to teach law?  

The lack of data skills among lawyers is now widely recognized as a problem, but law schools are not particularly well-equipped to teach either coding or data science, while undergraduate schools already teach these subjects.  As the business prospects of law firms and young lawyers are seen as under pressure, law schools also come under pressure.  An orientation toward “experiential” learning (for instance clinics and internships) and cross-disciplinary learning (decision-making, statistics, economics, visualization, design) can round out their experience.  The requirements of bar exams and the expectations of potential employers are a power force, and their appreciation of these broader skills is uneven.  

Conversely, computer science majors are often receptive to learning legal thinking and systems, and able to suggest improvements.  A subsisting problem is the values that systems should implement.  Legal thinking may have important lessons to teach about simple or optimistic solutions, about edge cases, “incompleteness” and systemic risk.
